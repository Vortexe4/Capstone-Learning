Standard Model of Particle Physics

The Standard Model explains how the smallest particles in the universe work, except for gravity. It talks about three main forces:

  • Electromagnetic Force: It affects charged particles like electrons and protons and is carried by photons. This force controls things like electricity and light.
  • Strong Force: It holds protons and neutrons together in the nucleus of atoms. Gluons carry this force and are the strongest force in the universe but work only over tiny distances.
  • Weak Force: It causes radioactive decay and is carried by W and Z bosons. This force helps power the Sun.

Particles:

  • Quarks: These make up protons and neutrons.
  • Leptons: Includes electrons and neutrinos.
  • Bosons: They carry forces, like photons for light and gluons for the strong force.
  • Higgs Boson: This particle explains why other particles have mass.

Limits of the Standard Model:

  • It doesn't explain gravity or the dark matter and dark energy that make up most of the universe.

Who created each model and when:

  • Max Planck (1858–1947)
  • Albert Einstein (1879–1955)
  • Niels Bohr (1885–1962)
  • Werner Heisenberg (1901–1976)
  • Paul Dirac (1902–1984)

This idea started as early as the 20th century, and the theory wasn’t confirmed until 2012 when the Higgs Boson confirmed it.

Created by: Max Planck, Albert Einstein, Niels Bohr, Werner Heisenberg, and Paul Dirac
